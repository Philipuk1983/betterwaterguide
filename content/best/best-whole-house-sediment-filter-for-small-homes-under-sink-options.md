## Start With the Sediment Problem

Sediment filtration is for particles: sand, silt, rust flakes, grit, and similar debris. It belongs at the beginning of a filtration setup because it keeps those particles from filling a more specialized filter farther down the line.

That is especially helpful when:

- Faucet aerators repeatedly collect rust-colored particles or grit.
- Water turns cloudy after plumbing work.
- Well water carries visible sediment.
- A carbon filter or reverse osmosis prefilter clogs much earlier than expected.
- A dedicated drinking-water system needs protection from particulate matter.

A sediment cartridge does not solve every water issue. It does not remove dissolved contaminants such as lead, nitrate, PFAS, hardness minerals, or chlorine. If water is clear but has a chlorine smell, metallic taste, staining, or another chemical concern, sediment filtration may still be useful as a first stage, but it is not the treatment answer on its own.

| Water issue or setup | Better starting point | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Fine particles, cloudy water, or clogged downstream filters | 5-micron sediment cartridge | Starting with a coarse cartridge when fine-particle protection is needed |
| Large visible grit that blocks fine cartridges quickly | 20-micron sediment cartridge | Using a 5-micron cartridge alone when it repeatedly loses flow |
| Existing 10-inch under-sink housing | A replacement cartridge suited to that housing | Buying a complete housing when only the cartridge needs replacement |
| Chlorine taste or odor | Carbon filtration | Expecting a sediment-only cartridge to improve taste |
| Sediment at showers, toilets, laundry, and kitchen fixtures | Main-line sediment filtration | Treating one sink and expecting whole-home coverage |

## Who Should Use an Under-Sink Sediment Filter?

This setup is for a kitchen sink or another single fixture where sediment is interfering with drinking-water filtration or causing visible debris at the faucet.

It suits small-home owners and renters who are allowed to modify plumbing, provided there is room beneath the sink for the housing, shutoff access, and cartridge changes. It is also a practical addition ahead of a dedicated drinking-water filter that does not need to spend its service life catching sand and rust particles.

Skip the under-sink route when sediment is affecting the entire home. If grit is reaching shower valves, washing machines, toilets, or water heaters, filtration belongs near the incoming water line. A compact filter beneath one sink cannot protect plumbing or fixtures elsewhere in the house.

## How the Picks Differ

The main decision is micron rating.

A 5-micron cartridge captures smaller sediment than a 20-micron cartridge. That makes 5 micron a better choice before a drinking-water filter, where fine particles can clog a carbon block or reverse osmosis prefilter. The trade-off is flow: fine media can become restrictive sooner when water contains a lot of sand or silt.

A 20-micron cartridge is a coarser first barrier. It is intended for larger, visible debris that would overwhelm a tighter cartridge. It will not provide the same fine-particle protection as a 5-micron filter, but it can be the more workable choice where coarse sediment is the dominant problem.

The DuPont cartridge is the simple replacement choice for a common 10-inch housing. The APEC CB1-MD is the housing choice for someone setting up a compact under-sink filtration point rather than replacing an existing cartridge.

## Before You Buy

### Match the Cartridge to the Housing

The cartridge and housing need to work together. A 10-inch label is important, but the cartridge still needs to seat and seal correctly in the housing used beneath the sink. The APEC O-Ring Sediment Filter is identified for APEC #CB1 and #CB2 housings. Use the cartridge style intended for the housing already installed.

If there is no housing yet, choose the housing first and plan the cartridge stage around it.

### Choose the Right Micron Rating

Use a 5-micron cartridge when fine sediment is the issue. This is the better route when a drinking-water filter needs protection from smaller particles.

Use a 20-micron cartridge when the water contains enough visible grit to clog a fine cartridge quickly. It is a coarse first barrier, not a replacement for fine filtration where fine-particle capture matters.

### Leave Room for Maintenance

Under-sink filters need more than enough room to hang from the cabinet wall. The lower portion of the housing must come off during a cartridge change.

Plan for:

- Clear space beneath the housing
- Access to the cold-water shutoff
- Room for a filter wrench
- A shallow pan or bowl to catch released water
- Towels for drips
- Enough open cabinet space to work without removing a disposal or drain assembly

### Watch for Flow Changes

A sediment cartridge does its work by collecting debris, so a gradual drop in cold-water flow is often the clearest sign that it is loading up. Keep a note inside the cabinet with the installation date and any noticeable change in flow or water appearance. That makes it easier to spot a pattern in your household rather than waiting until the faucet slows dramatically.

## Who Should Skip This Type of Filter?

Skip an under-sink whole-house-style sediment filter when water treatment is needed throughout the house. It will not protect shower fixtures, laundry appliances, toilets, water heaters, or any faucet outside the line that feeds the filtered sink.

Homes with severe sand or well-water debris should not rely on a compact under-sink filter as the primary sediment solution. A main-line sediment filter handles the problem before particles move through household plumbing.

Sediment filtration also is not the right single answer for chlorine, lead, PFAS, nitrate, hardness, bacteria, or dissolved metals. Those concerns require treatment selected for the specific issue.

Renters should consider whether permanent plumbing changes are allowed before installing a housing. A countertop filter, faucet-mounted filter, or pitcher is less suited to heavy sediment, but it avoids altering the supply line.

## FAQ

### Is an under-sink whole-house-style sediment filter a true whole-house filter?

No. An under-sink whole-house-style housing uses a cartridge format often associated with whole-house filtration, but it treats water at one sink. A true whole-house sediment filter is installed on the incoming water line before water reaches household fixtures.

### Should I choose a 5-micron or 20-micron sediment cartridge?

Choose 5 micron for finer sediment protection before a drinking-water filter. Choose 20 micron for heavy visible grit that causes a fine cartridge to clog and reduce flow quickly. A lower micron number captures smaller particles, while a higher micron number is coarser.

### Will a sediment cartridge improve chlorine taste or odor?

No. Sediment cartridges target particles such as sand, silt, and rust. Chlorine taste and odor require a treatment stage designed for chlorine reduction, such as carbon filtration.

### When should I replace an under-sink sediment cartridge?

Replace it when cold-water flow drops noticeably, when the cartridge is visibly loaded during servicing, or when sediment begins reaching the faucet aerator or downstream filter. Tracking the installation date and flow changes helps establish a replacement pattern for the sink.

### Can a sediment filter housing fit under a sink with a garbage disposal?

It can, provided there is clear room for the housing, cartridge removal, a filter wrench, and a small catch pan. Do not place the housing where disposal components, drain pipes, the cabinet floor, or stored items block access during a cartridge change.
