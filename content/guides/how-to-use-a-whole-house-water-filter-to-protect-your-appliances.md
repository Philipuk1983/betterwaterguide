For cartridge filters, install pressure gauges before and after the housing. Replace a sediment cartridge when the pressure drop reaches about 10 PSI, or earlier if showers weaken or appliances begin filling slowly.

## Start With a Water Test

A whole-house filter only addresses the problem its media is designed to handle. Sediment, hardness, iron, chlorine, and bacteria require different treatment methods, so begin with the water rather than with a filter housing.

For municipal water, read the utility's annual water-quality report and test water from a household tap. Water can pick up sediment, metals, or other issues after it enters the home, particularly in older plumbing.

For private wells, use a certified laboratory test at least annually for total coliform bacteria, nitrate, pH, and total dissolved solids. Include hardness, iron, manganese, and turbidity when you see orange staining, white scale, clogged screens, or recurring sediment in fixtures and appliances.

Build the treatment train in the order the water needs it:

1. **Remove large debris first.** A spin-down filter or coarse sediment stage catches sand, grit, and pipe debris.
2. **Add fine sediment filtration next.** A 5-micron cartridge is a useful final stage for fine rust and sediment.
3. **Treat iron or manganese before fine cartridges.** Dissolved metals need equipment selected for the water chemistry.
4. **Use carbon after sediment control.** Carbon media works better when it is not carrying a heavy load of grit.
5. **Address hardness when scale is the problem.** A softener or scale-control system protects water heaters, dishwashers, ice makers, humidifiers, and washing machines from mineral buildup.

A test strip can provide a quick hardness screen, but it cannot size iron treatment, identify bacteria, or explain a staining problem.

A 5-micron filter is a useful final barrier for fine debris, but it is rarely the right first stage for water carrying sand or heavy rust. That water can clog a fine cartridge quickly. A coarse prefilter ahead of the finer stage helps preserve flow and reduces cartridge changes.

Carbon has limits as well. Standard carbon media does not soften water. Chloramine reduction requires media and service flow designed for chloramine treatment. Certification can help narrow the field: NSF/ANSI 42 covers chlorine-related aesthetic claims, while NSF/ANSI 44 applies to residential water softeners.

## Hardness, Iron, Chlorine, and Household Flow

Hard water and sediment may create similar appliance headaches, but they need different treatment.

Sediment is made of particles. It can clog washing-machine screens, appliance inlet valves, faucet aerators, and fine filter cartridges. Hardness minerals are dissolved in clear water. They form scale inside water heaters, dishwashers, tankless heat exchangers, humidifiers, and ice makers.

One grain per gallon equals 17.1 mg/L as calcium carbonate. At 7 grains per gallon—about 120 mg/L—scale becomes a significant appliance concern. A softener removes hardness minerals. TAC changes mineral behavior to reduce scale formation, but it does not remove hardness from the water.

Choose a softener when the goal is to reduce scale and improve soap performance in laundry and dishwashing. TAC suits households that want scale reduction without salt handling or brine discharge and do not need softened water.

Iron can change the order of the whole installation. The common secondary drinking-water standard for iron is 0.3 mg/L, a level associated with staining and metallic taste. Iron can also clog fine filters and foul softening resin. Treat it as an iron problem before relying on a basic cartridge setup.

Flow rate matters just as much as water chemistry. A system that works during one shower may restrict water when two showers, a washing machine, and a dishwasher run at the same time. Add the flow rates of fixtures likely to operate together, then choose equipment with a documented service flow that supports that demand without excessive pressure drop.

## Common Whole-House Treatment Setups

Keep the system as simple as the water allows. Every additional stage adds maintenance, plumbing connections, and another possible source of pressure loss.

### City Water With White Scale and Dishwasher Spotting

Test hardness first. When hardness reaches 7 grains per gallon or higher, a softener is the direct route for protecting appliances from scale. Add sediment filtration only when rust flakes, grit, or visible debris are reaching fixtures.

### City Water With Chlorine Odor but No Scale

Use sediment filtration only if particles are present. Follow it with properly sized carbon treatment for chlorine reduction. Carbon addresses chlorine, odor, and taste concerns; it does not prevent mineral scale.

### Well Water With Grit in Toilet Tanks or Washing-Machine Screens

Start with coarse sediment removal, then add a finer cartridge stage. A clear housing or pressure gauges can show when sediment is building up and restricting flow.

### Well Water With Orange Staining or Metallic Water

Get laboratory results for iron, manganese, pH, and hardness before selecting treatment. Replacing fine cartridges repeatedly does not solve dissolved iron and creates unnecessary maintenance.

### Tankless Water Heater With Repeated Scale Warnings

Prioritize hardness treatment and follow the heater manufacturer's flushing instructions. Sediment filtration alone does not stop calcium scale inside a heat exchanger.

## Install the System for Service and Maintenance

Main-line equipment needs room around it. A cramped installation makes cartridge changes messy, encourages skipped maintenance, and can leave the home without water during service.

Plan for:

- Main-line placement before the water heater and appliance branches
- Shutoff valves on both sides of the treatment equipment
- A bypass valve for maintenance and troubleshooting
- Pressure gauges before and after filtration
- Enough vertical clearance to remove filter housings
- A nearby drain for softeners, backwashing filters, and some iron-treatment systems
- Freeze protection for equipment in garages, crawlspaces, and exterior utility areas

Keep ordinary whole-house filtration on the cold-water main. Installing a standard filter after the water heater can expose the housing and media to temperatures they are not designed to handle.

A bypass valve is especially useful during maintenance. It keeps water available while a cartridge is changed and helps isolate whether a pressure problem comes from the filter, plumbing, or the home's pressure system.

## Maintain the System Before Flow Drops

Do not rely on cartridge appearance alone. A cartridge can look fairly clean while creating enough internal restriction to weaken showers, slow appliance fill cycles, and reduce hose-bib flow.

After installing a new cartridge, record the clean pressure difference between the two gauges. Replace the cartridge when the difference reaches about 10 PSI. Change it sooner if household water pressure becomes unacceptable.

A simple maintenance routine helps prevent surprises:

- Write the installation date and clean pressure reading on each filter housing.
- Keep a sealed replacement cartridge indoors, away from freezing temperatures and direct sunlight.
- Shut off water before opening a housing, and keep a bucket and towels nearby.
- Inspect housing O-rings during each cartridge change.
- Lubricate O-rings only with the lubricant specified for the system.
- Sanitize filter housings during scheduled cartridge changes when the installation instructions call for it.
- Put softeners and backwashing systems into bypass before servicing connected treatment equipment.

## When a Basic Whole-House Filter Is Not Enough

A basic cartridge system cannot solve every water problem. Use a different treatment route when the issue involves source contamination, drinking-water treatment, or a plumbing failure.

Choose another approach when:

- **Lead is present at a drinking-water tap.** Lead often enters water through plumbing materials. A certified point-of-use lead filter and plumbing correction are more direct solutions.
- **A private well has bacterial contamination.** Source correction and properly designed disinfection are needed.
- **Nitrate, high sodium, or elevated total dissolved solids affect drinking water.** Reverse osmosis at the drinking-water tap is a more targeted option.
- **Water pressure is poor before filtration.** Repair well-pump, pressure-tank, plumbing, or supply problems before adding restrictive media.
- **The home is rented and main-line plumbing changes are not approved.** A point-of-use approach may be more appropriate.

## Mistakes That Cause Pressure Loss and Continued Scale

### Treating a Symptom Without Testing the Water

White scale, orange staining, chlorine odor, and grit do not point to the same treatment. A water test prevents a system built around the wrong problem.

### Using TDS as a Hardness Reading

Total dissolved solids includes many dissolved substances. It does not show how much calcium and magnesium are causing scale.

### Installing a 1-Micron Cartridge as the First Stage

Fine media catches fine debris, but it also loads quickly in visibly dirty water. Use staged filtration when sediment is heavy: coarse first, fine second.

### Placing Treatment After the Water Heater

If the goal is appliance protection, treated water needs to reach the water heater and appliance supply lines before scale or sediment can enter them.

### Skipping Pressure Gauges and a Bypass

Without gauges, reduced pressure can be difficult to trace. Without a bypass, even a routine cartridge change can interrupt water to the entire home.

## Bottom Line

Install whole-house treatment at the main water entry point, ahead of the water heater and appliance branches. Use sediment filtration for grit, carbon for chlorine-related concerns, iron treatment for staining metals, and hardness treatment for scale.

For a sediment filter, pressure gauges provide the clearest maintenance signal: replace the cartridge at about a 10-PSI pressure drop. A properly ordered treatment train protects appliances without turning filter changes into a constant chore.

## FAQ

### What micron filter protects appliances best?

A 5-micron sediment filter is a useful final stage for protecting appliance inlet screens, valves, and fill mechanisms from fine debris. Where water contains visible sand, rust flakes, or heavy sediment, use a 20- to 50-micron prefilter ahead of it.

### Will a whole-house filter stop water-heater scale?

Not unless the system includes hardness treatment. Sediment filtration removes particles, while a softener removes the dissolved calcium and magnesium that form scale on heating elements and inside tankless heat exchangers.

### How much pressure drop means a cartridge needs replacement?

Replace a cartridge when pressure gauges show about a 10-PSI drop across the filter housing. Change it sooner when showers weaken, appliances fill slowly, or the system reaches its stated service interval.

### Does a whole-house carbon filter soften water?

No. Carbon can reduce selected chemical contaminants, chlorine, odor, and taste concerns when the media and flow rate match the treatment claim. It does not remove hardness minerals or prevent mineral scale on its own.

### Do I need a plumber to install a whole-house filtration system?

Main-line installation is a plumbing project when it involves cutting pipe, adding shutoff valves, installing a bypass, or connecting equipment to a drain. A qualified installer can also help prevent pressure-loss problems and protect the system from freezing or improper drainage.
