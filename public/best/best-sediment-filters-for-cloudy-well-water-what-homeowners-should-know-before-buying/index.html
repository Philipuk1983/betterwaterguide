How to choose between 5 micron and 20 micron

The right micron rating is not about picking the smallest number. It is about choosing the level of capture that matches how dirty the well water really is.

  • Pick 5 micron when the water looks cloudy or hazy and you want finer sediment capture at one tap or across the whole house.
  • Pick 20 micron when you want a lighter first pass at the main line and do not want a cartridge that loads too fast.
  • Add a coarse prefilter or separator when the well sends sand, especially after storms or well service.
  • Put sediment filtration before carbon or reverse osmosis so grit does not shorten the life of the next stage.

A simple glass test can help. Fill a clear glass and let it sit for a few minutes. If particles settle, sediment is the likely problem. If the water stays orange, smells like sulfur, feels slimy, or leaves staining, a sediment cartridge is only part of the answer.

What cloudy well water is telling you

Cloudy water does not always mean the same thing. The symptom tells you where to start.

  • Cloudiness that settles in a glass points to suspended sediment.
  • Cloudiness at every faucet points to a whole-house problem.
  • Grit in aerators and appliance screens points to main-line sediment control.
  • Orange staining or a metallic taste points beyond plain sediment and toward iron or manganese.
  • Sulfur smell or a slimy feel points to a different treatment path.

That is why a sediment filter should be treated as a targeted fix, not a catch-all. It does one job well: removing particles that are floating in the water. It does not solve every well-water issue by itself.

If the cloudiness stays at one drinking tap, the APEC CF-5 and iSpring IS-5SD are the easiest under-sink starts. If the whole house looks cloudy, the DuPont whole-house cartridge is the better starting point. If the kitchen already uses reverse osmosis, the Home Master cartridge fits that line. If you want a lighter main-line first stage, the Pleasant Hearth 20-micron cartridge is the simpler entry point.

The bigger question is not which brand name sounds strongest. It is whether your water needs a single-tap cartridge, a whole-house cartridge, or a coarse first stage before a finer filter. Get that part right, and the rest of the choice becomes much easier.
